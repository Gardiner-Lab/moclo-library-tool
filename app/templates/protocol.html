MoClo Assembly Protocol

Modular Cloning: A hierarchical system for building complex genetic constructs

What is MoClo?

Modular Cloning (MoClo) is a standardized cloning system that enables rapid assembly of multiple DNA parts into complex genetic constructs. The system uses Type IIS restriction enzymes (like BsaI and BpiI) to create specific 4-base pair overhangs that ensure parts are assembled in the correct order and orientation.

Key Advantage: MoClo allows you to build increasingly complex constructs through a hierarchical assembly process, from individual parts (Level 0) to multi-gene pathways (Level 2 and beyond).

Level 0: Basic Parts

Level 0 parts are the fundamental building blocks of MoClo. Each part is flanked by BsaI recognition sites that generate specific 4-bp overhangs defining the part's position in the final construct.

Creating Level 0 Parts

  1. Design your genetic element (promoter, CDS, terminator, etc.)
  2. Add appropriate BsaI sites with correct overhangs for the part type
  3. Clone into a Level 0 entry vector
  4. Verify by colony PCR or restriction digest and then whole plasmid sequencing

Level 1: Transcriptional Cassettes

Level 1 assembly combines multiple Level 0 parts into a single transcriptional cassette (TC). BsaI digestion removes the entry vector backbone and creates compatible overhangs that ensure parts assemble in the correct order. The assembled TC is cloned into a Level 1 vector with BpiI sites for further assembly.

Level 1 Assembly Protocol

  1. Select Level 0 parts with compatible overhangs (e.g., Promoter → 5'UTR → CDS → Terminator)
  2. Mix parts with Level 1 destination vector, BsaI enzyme, and ligase
  3. Run one-pot reaction: cycles of digestion (37°C) and ligation (16°C)
  4. Transform into competent cells and select on appropriate antibiotic
  5. Verify assembly by colony PCR or restriction digest and then whole plasmid sequencing
Important: Level 1 vectors have different BpiI overhangs (positions A-G) that determine where the TC will be placed in Level 2 assembly. Choose the appropriate Level 1 vector for your desired final arrangement.

Level 2: Multi-Gene Constructs

Level 2 assembly combines multiple Level 1 transcriptional cassettes into a single plasmid. BpiI digestion creates position-specific overhangs (A-G) that ensure TCs are assembled in the correct order. This allows you to build complex multi-gene pathways or expression systems.

Laboratory Protocol

This section provides detailed laboratory procedures for performing GoldenGate-mediated MoClo assembly reactions. The protocol uses a one-pot reaction combining restriction digestion and ligation for efficient DNA assembly.

Materials Required

🧪 Reagents

  • 10x Buffer G (Thermo)
  • 100 mM ATP (Thermo)
  • BsaI or BpiI restriction enzyme (Thermo)
  • T4 DNA Ligase (Thermo)
  • Molecular-grade water (MQ)
  • DNA parts and vectors

🔬 Equipment

  • PCR machine/thermal cycler
  • PCR tubes (0.2 mL)
  • Ice bucket and ice
  • Pipettes and sterile tips
  • Centrifuge (optional)
  • Vortex mixer (optional)

⚠️ Safety Equipment

  • Lab coat
  • Gloves
  • Safety glasses

Handle restriction enzymes with care. Follow proper lab safety protocols.

Reaction Setup

Standard 20 µL Reaction

Component Volume Final Concentration Notes
10x Buffer G 2 µL 1x Thermo Fisher
100 mM ATP 0.2 µL 1 mM Thermo Fisher
Acceptor Vector X µL ~40 fmol Calculate based on size
Insert DNA(s) Y µL ~80 fmol each 2:1 molar ratio (insert:vector)
BsaI or BpiI 1 µL 10 units BsaI for Level 1, BpiI for Level 2
T4 DNA Ligase 0.4 µL 2 units Thermo Fisher
MQ Water to 20 µL - Molecular grade
Molar Ratio Calculation: Use a 2:1 molar ratio of insert to vector (80 fmol insert : 40 fmol vector). For multiple inserts, use 80 fmol of each insert. Calculate DNA amounts using the formula: ng = (fmol × bp × 660) / 1,000,000

Thermal Cycling Program

Step Temperature Time Cycles Purpose
1 37°C 10 min 60 cycles Digestion (BsaI/BpiI)
2 22°C 10 min Ligation (T4 Ligase)
3 37°C 10 min 1 cycle Final digestion
4 65°C 20 min 1 cycle Enzyme inactivation
5 12°C Hold - Storage until transformation
Troubleshooting: For difficult assemblies with many inserts, increase the cycle number from 60 to 80-100 cycles to improve assembly efficiency.

Step-by-Step Procedure

1

Plan Your Assembly

  • Design your construct using this MoClo assembly tool
  • Verify part compatibility and overhang sequences
  • For plant transformation, include a selection marker (e.g., BASTA resistance)
  • Place resistance cassette next to the Left T-DNA border
  • Check vector suitability for Agrobacterium (ORI in Agro)
2

Prepare Reaction on Ice

  • Thaw all components on ice
  • Label PCR tubes clearly
  • Add components in order: buffer, ATP, DNA, enzyme, ligase, water
  • Mix gently by pipetting or brief vortex
  • Spin down briefly to collect liquid
3

Run Thermal Cycling

  • Place tubes in thermal cycler
  • Start the MoClo program (60 cycles)
  • Total run time: ~20 hours
  • Can be run overnight
4

Transform E. coli

  • Use 5-10 µL of reaction for transformation
  • Transform into competent E. coli cells
  • Plate on selective media (appropriate antibiotic)
  • Incubate overnight at 37°C
5

Screen and Verify

  • Pick 4-8 colonies for screening
  • Perform colony PCR or miniprep + restriction digest for initial screening
  • Verify correct assembly by whole plasmid sequencing (e.g., Nanopore, PacBio, or Illumina)
  • Whole plasmid sequencing confirms complete construct integrity and detects any unexpected mutations

Storage and Disposal

📦 Storage

  • Enzymes: Store at -20°C
  • Reaction mix: 4°C (short-term) or -20°C (long-term)
  • Transformed cells: Glycerol stocks at -80°C
  • Plasmids: -20°C in TE buffer or water

🗑️ Disposal

  • Inactivate enzymes by autoclaving before disposal
  • Decontaminate bacterial cultures before discarding
  • Follow institutional biohazardous waste guidelines
  • Dispose of tips and tubes in biohazard containers

Important Notes for Plant Transformation

  • Binary Vector Design: For stable plant transformation, ensure your Level 2 construct is in a binary vector with T-DNA borders
  • Selection Marker: Include a plant resistance cassette (BASTA preferred for easy visual screening)
  • Marker Placement: Position the resistance cassette next to the Left T-DNA border to ensure only plants with complete insertions are resistant
  • Agrobacterium Compatibility: Verify the vector contains an appropriate origin of replication for Agrobacterium (ORI in Agro)

Tips & Best Practices

✓

Plan Your Assembly

Design your complete construct before starting. Map out which parts go into which Level 1 vectors and their final positions in Level 2.

✓

Check Overhangs

Always verify that adjacent parts have compatible overhangs. Mismatched overhangs will prevent assembly.

✓

Avoid Internal Sites

Ensure your parts don't contain internal BsaI or BpiI sites. These will interfere with assembly.

✓

Use Controls

Include positive and negative controls in your assembly reactions to troubleshoot failed assemblies.

✓

Sequence Verification

Always verify your final constructs with whole plasmid sequencing (Nanopore, PacBio, or Illumina) to confirm complete construct integrity and detect any unexpected mutations.

✓

Keep Records

Document your assemblies thoroughly. This tool helps you track parts, cassettes, and final plasmids.

⚠️

Frameshift Considerations

When designing parts that fuse to coding sequences, the 4bp overhang must be considered to avoid frameshifts. The last base pair of the overhang needs to be included in the last amino acid triplet of your coding sequence.

📝

Intron Annotation

If your coding sequences contain introns, proper GenBank annotation is essential for accurate translation analysis. Learn about annotation best practices →

How This Digital Assembly Tool Works

×

What You Need to Input

To use this tool effectively, you'll need to provide:

  • GenBank Files: Upload your DNA parts, backbones, and cassettes in GenBank (.gb) format
  • Part Sequences: Each part must contain the DNA sequence with proper annotations
  • Overhang Information: Parts must have compatible 4-base overhangs (e.g., GGAG, AATG, GCTT) for proper assembly
  • Part Metadata: Optional information like part type, function, organism, and references

Digital Assembly Process

When you assemble parts, the tool performs a complete in silico (digital) simulation of the MoClo assembly reaction:

1

Restriction Enzyme Simulation

The tool digitally "cuts" your parts using Type IIS restriction enzymes (BsaI for Level 0→1, BpiI for Level 1→2). It identifies restriction sites in the sequence and simulates the cutting pattern, exposing the 4-base overhangs.

2

Overhang Compatibility Check

The tool validates that adjacent parts have matching overhangs. For example, if Part A has a 3' overhang of AATG, Part B must have a 5' overhang of AATG to ligate properly. This ensures correct part order and orientation.

3

DNA Sequence Assembly

Once validated, the tool concatenates the DNA sequences in the correct order. It removes the restriction sites and fusion sequences, joining parts at their overhangs to create a seamless final sequence—exactly as would happen in the lab.

4

Feature Merging & Position Calculation

The tool tracks all genetic features (promoters, CDSs, terminators, etc.) from the original parts and recalculates their positions in the final plasmid. Features are preserved with their annotations, colors, and metadata.

5

Backbone Integration

For Level 1 and Level 2 assemblies, the tool inserts your assembled cassette into the selected backbone vector at the designated insertion slots. The final plasmid includes both your custom parts and the backbone features (origin of replication, antibiotic resistance, etc.).

Complete Workflow

  1. Upload Parts: Import GenBank files for your DNA parts via the Parts Browser
  2. Create Cassettes: Assemble Level 0 parts into transcriptional cassettes (Level 1)
  3. Select Backbone: Choose a compatible acceptor vector for your cassette
  4. Simulate Assembly: Preview the final plasmid sequence and features
  5. Validate: Check for restriction site conflicts and compatibility issues
  6. Export: Download your final plasmid as GenBank, FASTA, or circular map (PNG/SVG)

Export Capabilities

Once your plasmid is assembled, you can export it in multiple formats:

  • GenBank (.gb): Complete sequence with all annotations, ready for ordering or further analysis
  • FASTA (.fasta): Plain sequence file for sequence alignment or primer design
  • Circular Map (PNG/SVG): Visual representation showing all features with color-coded annotations
💡 Key Advantage: This digital approach lets you design, validate, and optimize your constructs before spending time and resources in the lab. You can test multiple designs, catch errors early, and ensure compatibility—all in silico.
